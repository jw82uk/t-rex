T-Rex anatomy

Unmatched Bone-Crushing Bite Force

While earlier predators sliced flesh, T. rex evolved to crush entirely through bone, boasting an estimated bite force of up to 12,800 pounds per square inch.

Think of it this way. It's the equivalent bite force of 4 of our biggest, strongest living crocodiles, or over 15,000 angry hamsters. Enough force to crush a car!

This allowed them to instantly shatter the defenses of armored prey and consume high-energy bone marrow.

The Tiny But Mighty Arms

People love to laugh at T. rex's tiny arms, but they were ridiculously strong! While they were too short to reach its own mouth, those arms were packed with thick muscles and tipped with two sharp claws.

A single T. rex arm could easily bicep-curl over 400 pounds. That means its tiny arm could lift an entire adult grizzly bear or 28 large pumpkins!

Scientists think that when a T. rex was wrestling with a large, angry dinosaur up close, it used these short, incredibly strong arms to pin its prey tightly against its chest so it couldn't wiggle away from those devastating jaws.

The Super-Spy Sensory System

The T. rex had the best sense of smell of any dinosaur to ever live because the part of its brain responsible for smell was absolutely massive. It could sniff out its prey from 10 miles away—that is like sitting at home and smelling a pizza being delivered in the next town over!

Also, unlike most predatory dinosaurs whose eyes faced sideways, T. rex had eyes that pointed straight forward—just like a hawk or a human. This gave it incredible "3D vision," allowing it to judge exactly how far away a running dinner was during a chase.

The Power-Walking Engines

An adult T. rex's legs were massive, over 3 metres tall. That's as tall as a single decker bus, or two average sized women!

But forget what you see in the movies—T. rex couldn't sprint fast enough to chase down a sports car. Its massive, muscular thighs were built for endurance. It could walk at a terrifyingly steady pace for hours without getting tired, slowly wearing down its prey.

Also, its feet were equipped with huge "shock-absorber" pads that cushioned its multi-ton weight, making this giant a fantastic ambush preadtor in the Cretaceous forests.

The Heavyweight Balance Beam

An adult T. rex's tail was about as long as a fully grown African elephant from trunk to tail, or two standard family cars parked end-to-end.

The T. rex's tail acted like a giant see-saw counterweight, perfectly balancing its heavy head, while the massive tail muscles anchored to the upper leg bones helped to propel the multi-tonne predator forward.

Additionally, the tail acted as a built-in steering rudder to help the T. rex pivot and turn quickly during a hunt.
